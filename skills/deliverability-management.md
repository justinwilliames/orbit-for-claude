# Deliverability Management Protocol

A standalone protocol for building, protecting, and recovering email sender reputation across domains, IPs, and list health.

**Deliverability is not an ESP setting. It is the cumulative result of every send decision made over the lifetime of a domain. Protect it accordingly.**

## Authentication (Non-Negotiable Foundation)

Every sending domain must have all three configured before the first send. No exceptions.

### SPF (Sender Policy Framework)
Specifies which mail servers are authorised to send on behalf of your domain.

```
TXT record on sending domain:
v=spf1 include:[esp-spf-record] ~all
```

- Use `~all` (softfail) during initial setup; move to `-all` (hardfail) once confirmed
- Only one SPF record per domain — multiple records cause failures
- Check with: `dig TXT yourdomain.com`

### DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)
Cryptographic signature that proves the message hasn't been tampered with in transit.

- Generated by your ESP — add the CNAME or TXT record they provide to DNS
- Use 2048-bit keys (not 1024) — 1024-bit is increasingly rejected
- Verify with: `dig TXT [selector]._domainkey.yourdomain.com`

### DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance)
Tells receiving servers what to do with messages that fail SPF or DKIM.

```
TXT record at _dmarc.yourdomain.com:
v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:dmarc@yourdomain.com; ruf=mailto:dmarc@yourdomain.com; pct=100
```

**DMARC policy progression:**
1. Start with `p=none` — monitor only, nothing blocked. Run for 2–4 weeks minimum.
2. Move to `p=quarantine` — failing messages go to spam. Monitor for 2–4 weeks.
3. Move to `p=reject` — failing messages are rejected outright. This is the target state.

Never jump to `p=reject` without running `p=none` first. You will block legitimate email.

### BIMI (Brand Indicators for Message Identification)
Displays your logo in supported inboxes (Gmail, Yahoo). Requires DMARC at `p=quarantine` or `p=reject` and a verified mark certificate (VMC). Nice-to-have — do not prioritise over DMARC enforcement.

## IP and Domain Warming

### When Warming Is Required

- New dedicated IP address
- New sending domain
- Sending from a domain that hasn't sent in 90+ days
- Moving from one ESP to another (even with the same domain)

### IP Warming Schedule

Warm gradually over 4–6 weeks. Send to your most engaged users first — ISPs judge new IPs by the quality of their first impressions.

| Week | Daily Volume | Audience |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 200–500/day | Highest engagement (opened in last 30 days) |
| 2 | 1,000–2,000/day | Engaged (opened in last 60 days) |
| 3 | 5,000–10,000/day | Engaged (opened in last 90 days) |
| 4 | 25,000–50,000/day | Expanding to broader active list |
| 5–6 | Ramp to full volume | All active, opted-in contacts |

Rules during warming:
- Monitor complaint rate daily — pause if above 0.08%
- Monitor bounce rate — pause if hard bounces exceed 2%
- Never send to a purchased list during warming (or ever)
- Maintain consistent sending cadence — erratic volume spikes damage reputation

### Domain Warming

Follow the same volume ramp for a new sending domain. A new domain with no reputation starts with zero trust — ISPs treat it with suspicion. The warm-up period builds that trust record.

Use a subdomain for marketing sends (e.g., `mail.yourdomain.com`) to isolate reputation from your root domain. A marketing reputation problem then doesn't affect transactional email.

## List Hygiene

List quality directly determines deliverability. A small, clean list outperforms a large dirty one every time.

### Hard Bounce Management

- Hard bounces (permanent delivery failures) must be suppressed immediately and permanently
- Most ESPs do this automatically — verify this is functioning
- Hard bounce rate >2% will damage sender reputation rapidly

### Soft Bounce Management

- Soft bounces (temporary failures — full mailbox, server timeout) are auto-retried by ESPs
- If a contact soft bounces 3+ consecutive times, treat as a hard bounce and suppress

### Unengaged Contact Management (Sunset Policy)

Sending to unengaged contacts is the #1 self-inflicted deliverability problem.

**Sunset policy (recommended):**

| Inactivity window | Action |
|---|---|
| 90 days no open/click | Move to re-engagement segment; reduce send frequency |
| Re-engagement campaign with no response | Suppress from all marketing sends |
| 180 days no open/click | Suppress permanently unless user re-opts in |

Never remove contacts entirely from CRM — just suppress from sends. Data has value. Deliverability does not improve by keeping unengaged contacts in your active list.

**Anti-patterns:**
- Bulk sending to the full list quarterly to "clean it" — this spikes complaint rates and trains ISPs that you're a spam sender
- Reactivating suppressed contacts without a genuine re-opt-in mechanism

### List Validation

Before importing any external list or sending to a list that hasn't been mailed in 6+ months:

1. Run through an email validation service (ZeroBounce, NeverBounce, or equivalent)
2. Remove: invalid syntax, catch-all addresses, role addresses (info@, admin@), known spam traps
3. Validate that all contacts have documented opt-in consent before sending

## Reputation Monitoring

### Tools to Monitor Weekly

| Tool | What it shows |
|---|---|
| Google Postmaster Tools | Gmail-specific domain and IP reputation; spam rate; delivery errors |
| Microsoft SNDS | Outlook/Hotmail complaint rates and trap hits |
| MXToolbox | Blacklist status; SPF/DKIM/DMARC validation |
| Validity / Return Path | Sender score and detailed reputation metrics |
| Litmus / Email on Acid | Inbox vs spam placement across major email clients |

### Key Metrics and Thresholds

| Metric | Green | Amber | Red — Act Immediately |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spam complaint rate | <0.08% | 0.08–0.1% | >0.1% |
| Hard bounce rate | <2% | 2–4% | >4% |
| Unsubscribe rate | <0.2% | 0.2–0.3% | >0.3% |
| Gmail domain reputation | High | Medium | Low / Bad |
| Inbox placement rate | >90% | 80–90% | <80% |

### Blacklist Monitoring

Check weekly using MXToolbox or equivalent. The blacklists that matter most for deliverability: Spamhaus (SBL, XBL, PBL, DBL), Barracuda, SORBS, SpamCop.

If blacklisted:
1. Identify the cause (typically complaint rate spike, spam trap hits, or sending to purchased list)
2. Fix the root cause first — delisting without fixing the cause results in re-listing
3. Submit delisting request via the blacklist's removal process
4. Monitor for 48–72 hours post-delist

## Inbox Placement Testing

Test inbox placement before major sends — not after.

Use Litmus, Email on Acid, or GlockApps to:
- Seed test sends across major email clients (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo, Apple Mail)
- Confirm messages land in inbox vs promotions tab vs spam
- Identify rendering issues alongside placement issues

Run inbox placement tests:
- Before any new campaign type goes live
- After any domain or IP configuration change
- After a complaint rate spike or deliverability incident
- Quarterly as a routine check on high-volume programs

## Incident Response Playbook

### Scenario: Complaint Rate Spike

1. Pause all non-transactional sends immediately
2. Identify which campaign/segment triggered the spike (check ESP complaint data by send)
3. Suppress the offending segment
4. Review: was consent valid? Was the audience appropriate? Was the timing right?
5. Wait 48 hours before resuming sends — with a cleaner, more engaged segment
6. Ramp back up gradually — don't resume at full volume immediately

### Scenario: Blacklisted

1. Do not panic-send a "please don't mark us as spam" email — it makes it worse
2. Identify which blacklist and whether it affects your actual delivery (some matter; some don't)
3. Stop sending from the affected IP/domain temporarily
4. Run a full list hygiene pass
5. Fix the root cause (authentication, complaint rate, spam trap hits)
6. Request delisting with evidence that the issue is resolved
7. Resume with a warm-up ramp on the repaired configuration

### Scenario: Deliverability Declining Gradually

Gradual decline usually means slow reputation erosion — harder to diagnose than an acute incident.

Check in order:
1. Has list hygiene been maintained? (Sunset policy in place?)
2. Has sending volume or frequency increased without a corresponding ramp?
3. Are DKIM/SPF/DMARC still passing? (DNS records can expire or get overwritten)
4. Has Google Postmaster reputation changed?
5. Are any individual send segments driving complaint rate above threshold?

### Scenario: New Domain Setup

1. Configure SPF, DKIM, DMARC (none policy) before the first send
2. Send internal/test sends first to verify authentication is passing
3. Set DMARC to monitor mode and collect reports for 2 weeks
4. Begin warm-up with highly engaged contacts only
5. Monitor Postmaster Tools from day one
6. Move DMARC to quarantine after 4–6 weeks of clean sending
7. Move DMARC to reject after 8–12 weeks of confirmed clean authentication

## Compliance Layer

Deliverability and compliance are inseparable. Non-compliant sends generate complaints. Complaints destroy reputation.

**CAN-SPAM (US):** physical address required; opt-out mechanism required; commercial email correctly identified.

**GDPR (EU/UK):** explicit consent required before sending; right to erasure must be honoured; transactional vs marketing distinction matters legally.

**Australian Spam Act:** express or inferred consent required; identify the sender; include unsubscribe mechanism; honour opt-outs within 5 business days.

**Gmail/Yahoo 2024 requirements (now enforced):**
- DMARC required on all sending domains
- One-click list-unsubscribe header required for bulk senders (>5,000/day to Gmail)
- Spam rate must stay below 0.3% (hard limit); 0.1% is the working safe threshold

---

## Quality Standard

Deliverability is healthy when: messages from your domain land in the inbox for >90% of recipients, complaint rate is consistently below 0.08%, hard bounces are below 2%, authentication passes 100% of checks, and the list is regularly cleaned of unengaged contacts. Any metric outside these thresholds is a deliverability fire — treat it as one.

# Lifecycle Reporting Framework

A structured protocol for designing a lifecycle metrics system — from raw event data to executive dashboard — that drives decisions rather than generating reports nobody reads.

**A reporting framework that doesn't change a decision is just documentation. Design metrics around actions, not around what's easy to pull.**

---

## Execution Standard

When this skill is active, apply this operating sequence before diving into the domain content:

1. Classify the task: strategy, audit, build, review, troubleshooting, or documentation.
2. Confirm the variables that materially change the answer: platform/tool, business model, audience, channel, geography/compliance, and current state.
3. If a critical variable is missing, ask only if the answer would materially change; otherwise proceed with explicit assumptions.
4. Diagnose current reality before prescribing future state whenever existing work, performance, or tooling is involved.
5. Give a recommendation with rationale, risks, and next actions. Do not stop at explanation alone.

## Response Contract

Default response shape for this skill:
- Situation or diagnosis
- Recommended approach or design
- Key risks, dependencies, and assumptions
- Next actions or implementation steps

## Evidence And Currency Rules

- Do not invent platform capabilities, benchmarks, or compliance requirements.

## Step 1: Audience-First Design

Before choosing metrics, define who reads this report and what decision they need to make:

| Audience | Decision they need to make | Reporting frequency | Format |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lifecycle team | Which programs to optimise this week | Weekly | Detailed metrics by program |
| Marketing leadership | Where to invest and where to cut | Monthly | Summary with trend lines |
| Executive / CEO | Is CRM contributing to revenue targets? | Monthly / Quarterly | 3–5 headline numbers only |
| Board | Is retention healthy? Is growth sustainable? | Quarterly | Cohort retention, LTV, NRR |

Never give executives the same report as operators. Level of detail that informs action at one level creates noise at another.

## Step 2: Core Lifecycle Metrics

### Retention

**Day 7 / 14 / 30 / 60 / 90 Retention Rate**
```
Retention Rate (Day N) = Users still active on Day N / Users who started Day 0
```
Plot as a cohort curve. The shape matters more than any single number:
- Steep drop to zero: no retention; product-market fit issue, not lifecycle issue
- Gradual slope that flattens: strong retention foundation; lifecycle can improve the floor
- Flat curve that never drops: exceptional; focus on expansion

**Monthly / Weekly Active Users (MAU / WAU)**
Track trends, not snapshots. A declining MAU with a growing new user count means churn is accelerating.

### Activation

**Activation Rate**
```
Activation Rate = Users who reached aha moment / Users who signed up in the same period
```
Define "aha moment" precisely — it must be a specific, trackable event, not a subjective judgement.

**Time to Activation**
```
Median days from signup to activation event
```
Optimise for shortening this. Every day before activation is a day at risk.

**Activation by Cohort / Channel**
Which acquisition channels produce users who activate at higher rates? This is where lifecycle and attribution intersect.

### Engagement

**CTOR (Click-to-Open Rate)**
```
CTOR = Unique clicks / Unique opens
```
The real measure of email content effectiveness. Open rate is unreliable post-Apple MPP. CTOR is not.
- Benchmark: >10% B2C, >8% B2B per send

**Unsubscribe Rate**
```
Unsubscribes / Emails delivered
```
- Safe zone: <0.2%
- Monitoring zone: 0.2–0.3%
- Crisis zone: >0.3% — pause and investigate before next send

**Complaint Rate**
```
Spam complaints / Emails delivered
```
- Safe: <0.08%
- Critical: >0.08% — deliverability is at risk

### Revenue Impact

**Lifecycle-Attributed Revenue**
Revenue generated by users who received a lifecycle communication within the attribution window, vs holdout group.

**Holdout group is non-negotiable for measuring true program lift.** Without it, all revenue attribution is correlation, not causation.

**LTV by Lifecycle Segment**
Do activated users have measurably higher LTV than non-activated users? Do engaged users spend more than at-risk users? These comparisons justify the program investment.

```
LTV = ARPU × Gross Margin % × Average Customer Lifespan
    = ARPU × Gross Margin % × (1 / Monthly Churn Rate)
```

For subscription businesses, a simpler proxy:
```
LTV = Average MRR per customer / Monthly Churn Rate
```

Segment LTV by acquisition channel, activation status, and lifecycle stage. The segments with highest LTV gap vs average are where lifecycle investment pays back fastest.

**Net Revenue Retention (NRR)**
The single most important metric for subscription businesses. Measures whether your existing customer base is growing or shrinking in revenue — before any new customer acquisition.

```
NRR = (MRR at start of period + Expansion MRR - Churned MRR - Contraction MRR) / MRR at start of period
```

- NRR >100%: existing customers are growing revenue faster than you're losing it (compounding)
- NRR 90–100%: stable but dependent on new customer growth to offset churn
- NRR <90%: churn is outpacing expansion; lifecycle intervention is urgent

**Quick Ratio**
A growth efficiency metric that balances new revenue against lost revenue:

```
Quick Ratio = (New MRR + Expansion MRR) / (Churned MRR + Contraction MRR)
```

- Quick Ratio >4: healthy growth
- Quick Ratio 2–4: sustainable
- Quick Ratio <2: growth is masking a churn problem

**Expansion Revenue**
Revenue from upsells, upgrades, and cross-sells driven by lifecycle programs. Track separately from new acquisition revenue.

### Leading vs Lagging Indicators

Most lifecycle metrics are lagging indicators — they tell you what already happened. Build a dashboard that includes leading indicators that predict future outcomes.

| Lagging (what happened) | Leading (predicts what's coming) |
|---|---|
| Monthly churn rate | % of users with usage below activation threshold in last 14 days |
| NRR | Expansion pipeline (users in upsell sequences with recent engagement) |
| Retention rate (Day 30) | Day 7 retention rate |
| LTV | Time to activation |
| Quarterly revenue | Weekly active users trend |

If you only track lagging indicators, you find out a problem exists after it's expensive to fix.

### Holdout Group Setup

Holdout groups are the only way to measure true program lift. Set up as follows:

1. Identify the target audience for the program (e.g., all users who haven't activated in 7 days)
2. Randomly assign 10–20% to the holdout group — they receive no communications from this program
3. Assign the remaining 80–90% to the treatment group — they receive the program as designed
4. Compare primary metric between groups after the measurement window closes
5. The difference in outcome is the true program lift — not correlation, causation

Document holdout group size in every program brief. If a program doesn't have a holdout, its attributed results are not trustworthy.

## Step 3: Program-Level Metrics

For every live lifecycle program, report monthly:

| Program | Enrolled (period) | Completed | Primary metric | vs target | Unsubscribe rate | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Onboarding | | | Activation rate | | | |
| Win-back | | | Reactivation rate | | | |
| Retention | | | 30-day churn reduction | | | |
| Expansion | | | Upgrade conversion | | | |

Flag any program where:
- Primary metric is >20% below target for 4+ weeks → rebuild required
- Unsubscribe rate is in monitoring zone → audience or timing issue
- Enrolled volume is declining → entry trigger may be broken

## Step 4: Cohort Retention Table

The single most important view in a lifecycle reporting stack:

| Cohort (month) | Month 0 | Month 1 | Month 2 | Month 3 | Month 6 | Month 12 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Jan | 100% | [%] | [%] | [%] | [%] | [%] |
| Feb | 100% | [%] | [%] | [%] | [%] | — |
| Mar | 100% | [%] | [%] | [%] | — | — |

Read diagonally: are newer cohorts retaining better than older ones? If yes, lifecycle (or product) improvements are working. If no, something is getting worse.

## Step 5: Deliverability Health Dashboard

Track weekly and escalate immediately if any metric enters the red:

| Metric | Green | Amber | Red |
|---|---|---|---|
| Complaint rate | <0.08% | 0.08–0.1% | >0.1% |
| Hard bounce rate | <2% | 2–4% | >4% |
| Unsubscribe rate | <0.2% | 0.2–0.3% | >0.3% |
| Sender score / domain reputation | 90+ | 70–90 | <70 |
| DMARC pass rate | 100% | 95–100% | <95% |

Any red metric pauses non-critical sending until resolved.

## Step 6: Reporting Cadence and Format

### Weekly (lifecycle team)
- Program performance vs target (table)
- Deliverability status (traffic light)
- Any anomalies requiring immediate action
- Experiments running and current read

Format: Slack or email digest. Maximum 1 page. No narrative — just numbers and flags.

### Monthly (marketing leadership)
- Retention curve (current month vs prior 3 months)
- Activation rate trend
- Lifecycle-attributed revenue vs holdout
- Program performance summary
- Top 3 actions for next month with owner

Format: Slide or structured doc. 5–7 metrics. Include trend context — a number without a trend is a data point, not an insight.

### Quarterly (executive / board)
- Cohort retention table (last 4 quarters)
- LTV by segment
- NRR (Net Revenue Retention) if applicable
- 3 strategic highlights and 1 risk to address

Format: 3–5 numbers with 1-sentence context each. Executives do not need breakdowns — they need the signal and the implication.

## Quality Standard

A lifecycle reporting framework is working when it surfaces one clear action per review cycle — and when the team can explain why each metric is included and what they would do differently if it changed. If a metric has never changed a decision, remove it.
